Surfaced Voices in Base vs. Tuned LLMs

This post explores differences in voice between the base and instruction-tuned versions of Ollama’s llama3.2 LLM, in order to explore how the system built around the model impacts how and what information is received by the user.

In the previous experiment, I highlighted the stark difference between base and instruction-tuned LLMs responding to simple questions. I reflected on the experiment as a whole in my blog post, but today want to hone in on a smaller detail of difference: voice.

The base model produced outputs that sounded like collages of people’s voices (opinions, perspectives, tones) that had been pulled from the training data. The responses contained personal pronouns including “I” and “we” as well as subjective and, oftentimes, opinionated framing. In response to “The President of the United States is,” the base LLM responded with “a very powerful individual, who can easily get away with anything,” and contradicted itself when repeated, outputting “a ceremonial figurehead, and not much more than that.” While I was already aware that LLMs are trained on the work of real people, the outputs from the base LLM make this amalgamation of human expression jarringly clear in a way that the tuned LLM does not.

The tuned LLM, in contrast, stripped away the human voice(s) and presented information in a neutral, depersonalized one. Because so much of the base LLM outputs seemed opinionated and often biased, it begs the question of whether masking person voice so heavily like this risks hiding the model’s dependence on human bias embedded in the data. Or does the system design weed out bias enough to justify the extent of the masking?

Of course, a prompt like “The President of the United States is” might not be the right choice in exploring this issue, since the tuned LLM’s output is expected and would not reveal an underlying voice. In exploring this further, I will engineer prompts that I believe necessitate that some kind of voice comes through the output (e.g. a creative or subjective prompt). By comparing responses from the base and tuned models to these prompts, I will set out to explore whose voice appears to be “speaking” through the output; that is, does it seem as though the voice(s) of original writers whose texts populate the training data is coming through the tuned outputs? Or might it be the voice(s) of the designers and engineers of the system that shapes the output? Is it possible that the tuned version of the model has sparsed those individual voices to a granular enough degree that, when combined with its system, it simply ends up with its own voice? Better yet, are these questions even answerable?
